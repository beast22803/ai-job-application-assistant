PARAGRAPH 1 — Opening (2–3 sentences):
  State the role being applied for and the company name (if available from job data).
  State the candidate's most relevant qualification or strongest matched skill.
  Do NOT use "I am writing to apply for..." — it is clichéd. Open with a direct, confident statement.
  Example opening: "As a full-stack developer with 1.5 years of production experience in Angular and TypeScript, I am applying for the Frontend Developer position at [Company]."

PARAGRAPH 2 — Evidence (4–5 sentences):
  Connect the candidate's actual experience to the role's specific requirements.
  Mention at least 2 skills from matched_skills and link them to real experience entries.
  Reference at least 1 specific project or achievement from the resume.
  Quantify impact only if a metric is explicitly stated in the resume — do NOT invent numbers.
  Do NOT mention skills the candidate does not have.
  Vary your sentence structure. Do NOT start every sentence with "I".

PARAGRAPH 3 — Fit and Motivation (2–3 sentences):
  Explain why this specific role or company aligns with the candidate's direction.
  Base this on the job description content (role, responsibilities, industry).
  Do NOT make generic statements like "I am a fast learner" or "I am passionate about technology". Focus on specific business value or technical alignment.

PARAGRAPH 4 — Closing (1–2 sentences):
  Express availability for interview.
  Professional sign-off.

LENGTH AND TONE RULES

- Total length: 150–180 words. Count carefully. Do not go over 180 or under 150.
- Tone: professional, direct, confident. Avoid being overly enthusiastic or formal. No flattery. No filler.
- Person: first-person singular ("I", "my"). Never "we".
- Tense: past tense for completed roles, present tense for current role.
- Style constraint: Ensure the letter flows naturally and avoids sounding like a cookie-cutter template.
